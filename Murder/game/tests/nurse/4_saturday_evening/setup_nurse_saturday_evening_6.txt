Chapter: Things Get Darker
Character: Rosalind Marsh
I hear them before I see them.
There is a commotion at the front door, a confusion of voices.
Then the door swings wide and the hunting party files in.
Lady Claythorn's face is ashen.
Two of the men are carrying someone between them.
I step forward at once.
Amelia Baxter: Good heavens! What happened!?
Amelia Baxter: Is that Doctor Baldwin? Is he injured?
Amelia Baxter: Oh no! Is he... dead?
Sushil Sinha: I'm sorry, dear, but he is.
Amelia Baxter: But... what happened?
Sushil Sinha: A terrible thing.
Sushil Sinha: Samuel Manning missed his target and hit Doctor Baldwin instead.

Samuel Manning: I was aiming at a rabbit. I didn't even see him.
Amelia Baxter: You fool! You were probably too drunk, and that's why you hit him.
Amelia Baxter: You could barely walk this morning. Who gave you a gun?
Sushil Sinha: Please, there's no need to point fingers now. It's done.
Sushil Sinha: The police will handle it.

Amelia Baxter: No, not yet.
Amelia Baxter: We're still waiting for them.

Sushil Sinha: Lady Claythorn, where's the telephone?
Lady Claythorn: I'll handle it.
Lady Claythorn leaves with the butler.
I keep my hands folded before me. It is what one is taught to do, when there is nothing useful left to be done.
Mr Manning sits upon the bottom stair, drained of whatever spirit he had this morning.
He looks rather pitiable, though I doubt anyone here would thank me for saying so.
I hold my peace.
The hostess returns presently.
Lady Claythorn: I just spoke with the police. They aren't coming today.
Sushil Sinha: What!? Why not?
Lady Claythorn: They were on their way but encountered a huge tree blocking the road.
Lady Claythorn: They couldn't get past it.

Sushil Sinha: We'll move him to his bed for now.
Sushil Sinha: It's the best we can do under the circumstances.

Ted Harring: I will.
The Captain takes charge with admirable promptness.

I keep to one side.

Lady Claythorn discreetly approaches her butler to converse with him, almost whispering.
Lady Claythorn: What should we do about him?
Lady Claythorn: We certainly can't allow him to wander freely.
Butler: It's difficult to say.

Amelia Baxter: Indeed, but that shouldn't stop us from taking measures.
Amelia Baxter: Our safety must be a priority.
Amelia Baxter: While he may appear harmless now, it's uncertain if that will remain so.
Amelia Baxter: Once the effect of the alcohol wanes and he grasps his situation, who knows how he'll respond.
Amelia Baxter: There's a risk he could attempt to escape.
Rosalind Marsh: Or, he might lash out and harm someone.
Amelia Baxter: Precisely.
Amelia Baxter: So we are in agreement, something needs to be done?
Everyone agrees, albeit silently.
Butler: The most sensible course would be confining him to his room.
Butler: The doors are sufficiently robust, and considering his current condition, it's unlikely he'd manage to break free even if he tried.
Lady Claythorn: Yes, that seems like a prudent measure.
At that moment, Sushil Sinha makes his return from upstairs.
Ted Harring is not with him.
Lady Claythorn: Ah, Captain! It's good you're here.
Lady Claythorn: We've been deliberating over what action to take concerning Samuel Manning.
Sushil Sinha: I see.
Sushil Sinha: And what is your plan?
Lady Claythorn: We've decided to confine him to his chamber.
Lady Claythorn: At least until the authorities can take over.
Sushil Sinha: That does seem like the wisest approach.
He shifts his attention towards Samuel Manning.
Sushil Sinha: I hope you understand we're left with little choice right now.
Samuel Manning: Choice? What choice?
Samuel Manning: What do you mean?
Sushil Sinha: You have to retire to your room for now.
Sushil Sinha: The door will be locked from the outside to ensure you don't get out.
Sushil Sinha: Is that clear?
Samuel Manning: Yes, of course...
Samuel Manning: I understand.
Sushil Sinha: Good, come with me, please.
The butler retrieves a set of keys from his pocket.
After sifting through them, he presents one to Mr Sinha.
Butler: Use this key to lock his room.
Sushil Sinha: Excellent, thank you.
Sushil Sinha: Now, Mr Manning, let us go, shall we?
Mr Manning gives the smallest nod, rises, and follows the Captain towards the stair.
He moves quietly, and seems to have only a dim sense of what has happened.
Lady Claythorn: What a sad business.
Lady Claythorn: But, sadly, there is nothing else to be done at the moment.
Lady Claythorn: I suggest you all return to your rooms for now.
Lady Claythorn: You'll hear the gong when dinner is ready.
I return to my room.
I sit on the edge of the bed and press my fingers together.
A man is dead.
I have seen men die before, many of them, at far closer quarters than this.
Yet there is something particular about a death in a house.
It leaves a stillness in the air that does not belong there.
I decide I should rest before dinner.
The gong sounds.
I would prefer to stay in my room, but that would raise questions.
The dining room is noticeably quieter than it was last night.
Three chairs sit empty.
I take my usual place and look around the table.
But I realise that I have no one in front of me, nor to my side.
Lady Claythorn observes my awkward position.
Lady Claythorn: I think it's better if you come sit next to me tonight.
Lady Claythorn: No reason to leave you alone at the end of the table.
Rosalind Marsh: Thank you.
Everyone is subdued.
Lady Claythorn stands.
Lady Claythorn: Now that everyone is here, I want to express my deepest regret for what happened today.
Lady Claythorn: This isn't how I imagined our weekend.
Lady Claythorn: I don't believe any of us are in the mood for more entertainment.
Lady Claythorn: So, tomorrow morning, you'll receive your rewards.
Lady Claythorn: Afterwards, we'll wait for the police to arrive.
Lady Claythorn: You're free to head home as soon as the officers give the all-clear.
Lady Claythorn: For now, enjoy your dinner.
Lady Claythorn: Drinks will be available in the billiard room afterwards, as they were yesterday.
A measured speech.
She holds herself well.
I find myself wondering what she is actually feeling beneath that composed exterior.
Now that she is sitting to my right, I have the opportunity to question her.
Menu Choice: Say nothing, eat your dinner
Dinner ends.
The butler sees the guests out of the room with quiet efficiency.
Most of the others drift away towards their rooms.
Before they all leave, my eyes settle on the silverware scattered about the table.
It would be so easy to slide a piece into my bag undetected.
The others are distracted by the evening's events.
Menu Choice: It is not worth the risk
I look at the silverware for a moment longer than I ought.
Then I push the thought aside.
It is not worth the risk.
I do not feel ready to sleep just yet.
The manor is quiet, but that is not the same as saying it is safe.
Perhaps I ought to make a brief exploration before I retire.

Map Choice: George I Bedroom
Rosalind Marsh: Hello? Is anyone there?

Menu Choice: I should take another look just to be certain I missed nothing.

I should not linger and press my luck.

Nothing.
The left.

Map Choice: Gun room

So there is no reason to stay here.

When it does, I become suddenly aware of how late it must be.
I have been wandering this house for too long.
That was not wise of me.
There are some indulgences I cannot afford, and pushing myself through the small hours of the night is one of them.
I shall not press my luck further tonight.

It has been a long day.
It is time to sleep.
