Chapter: The Hunt
Character: Thomas Moody
I return to my room to change into the tweeds the household has laid out for me.
My hands are steady as I dress. Steadier than they have any right to be.
The letter still lies on my bedside table.
If I am to believe it, Captain Sinha put his name to the order that sent Tom up to the line.
Because of that, Tom came back from the war behind a mask, and lived a small and lonely life until his wounds got the better of him.
A thought occurs to me, one I do not like but cannot ignore.
And a hunt is a careless sort of business.
Accidents happen.
The butler is in the gun room, attending the rifles with his unhurried care.
He hands me a rifle.
It has been years since I last held a rifle, yet it settles into my hands like an old habit.
I check the action, sight along the barrel, and find it true.
I shall not embarrass myself today.
The others are already gathering on the lawn.
Two different groups are forming: Captain Sinha with Lady Claythorn and Samuel Manning with Doctor Baldwin.
It looks like it is up to me to decide which party I will join.
My first instinct is to follow Captain Sinha.
I simply cannot ignore the letter.
Being close to him will allow me to learn more of it.
Yet, I do not trust what is happening here this weekend.
The letter, the hunt, the surprise the maid could not put a name to.
Making me follow the captain is probably the goal of whoever organised this.
So, which group should I join?
Menu Choice: Lady Claythorn and Captain Sinha
Now that the groups are set, we make our way into the woods.
We walk some distance before I spot a pheasant, not ten yards off our line.
I have it before the Captain's rifle is even at his shoulder.
The bird drops cleanly.
Lady Claythorn: Bravo, Mr Moody. A splendid shot.
Thomas Moody: You flatter me, my lady.
Thomas Moody: One does one's best.
The Captain's turn comes a few minutes on, at a rabbit sat in the grass.
He misses by a yard, and not for want of a steady morning.
A decorated soldier, and he cannot put a ball into a rabbit sitting still in the open.
I had wondered at it. Now I am sure.
Whatever he was in the war, it was not what he tells the dinner table.
Thomas Moody: Hard luck, Captain. Hard luck indeed.
Sushil Sinha: I misjudged the lead.
Thomas Moody: Quite. The lead.
I let a little of the mockery show. I cannot help myself.
So this is the great Captain Sinha. A man who cannot strike a rabbit at twenty paces, who holds forth each evening on Burma and the Boxers as though he had won the Empire single-handed.
A counterfeit hero in a borrowed reputation.
Someone who sent others to the guns, and then dined out, year upon year, on a war he never once smelt.
The anger rises in my throat like bile.
By the time the butler calls us in for luncheon, only I have any game to show for the morning.
Lady Claythorn: Three birds and a pair of rabbits, Mr Moody. You are most impressive.
Lady Claythorn: I confess I had no notion we should be so splendidly provided for.
Thomas Moody: Your ladyship is too generous.
Thomas Moody: I was just very lucky today, that is all.
Thomas Moody: Otherwise, I am certain a decorated veteran like Captain Sinha would have put me quite to shame.
The Captain says nothing to that. He only smiles, thin and correct.
We settle for luncheon in a clearing among the birches. The butler lays a cloth and pours the tea with his customary care.
Lady Claythorn: Captain, I have been told you are an amazing story teller.
Lady Claythorn: Isn't there a story you would like to share with us.
Sushil Sinha: There is little worth the telling, my lady.
Sushil Sinha: But if it would amuse you, I might tell you about the "War of the Golden Stool", one of my first campaigns.
Lady Claythorn: What an intriguing name, please do tell.
So he does, modest and precise, with the ease of a man who has told it across a hundred dinner tables.
Lady Claythorn leans in, delighted.
I, on the other hand, feels the anger rising as the story progress.
If the letter is correct, and I have started to accept it is, this man was always safe behind a desk.
But he is playing the hero, as if he was the sole responsible for England's victory.
My thumb finds the cold of the trigger guard where the rifle rests across my knee.
And still the maid's words will not leave me. A surprise, prepared for the guests.
The letter left in my room. The hunt laid on the very next morning.
The Captain, set before me like a target on a wall.
It is too obvious that someone is trying to use me.
Menu Choice: Spare him
No, I cannot kill the man.
Even if he is a liar, a fraud, what is happening here is too suspicious.
I let the rage go cold in my hands.
I let the Captain finish is story.
Whoever drew us here wanted him dead by my hand, or more likely by Thomas'
That is the thing I must focus on.
The thought has scarcely formed when a shot cracks from the direction of the other party.
Then, thin between the trees, a cry.
My blood runs cold.
Before I can move, the Captain is on his feet and away, crashing through the undergrowth toward the sound far faster than I would have given him credit for..
I go after the Captain as best I can, the butler is close behind me.
The captain is faster that I would have guessed, the gap between us widening with every stride.
After a while I have to stop to catch my breath.
The butler stops with me.
Butler: Not that way, sir.
Something hard presses itself between my shoulders, and I understand, far too late, that I was never meant to reach the other party at all.
Ahead of me, the Captain runs on, and does not look back, and never knows.
You would not be made a murderer, and that was to your credit.
But sparing the Captain did not spare you.
