Chapter: The Hunt
Character: Thomas Moody
I return to my room to change into the tweeds the household has laid out for me.
My hands are steady as I dress. Steadier than they have any right to be.
The letter is folded in my breast pocket, where I can feel the small weight of it against my chest.
All night I turned it over, and all night the answer came back the same.
Captain Sinha put his name to the order that sent Tom up to the line.
Tom came back from it behind a mask, and lived a small and lonely life, and died of those wounds in his bed years after the guns fell silent.
Whatever else is false in this house, that much I am certain of.
And a hunt is a careless sort of business. Accidents happen. Everyone says so.
The butler is in the gun room, attending the rifles with his unhurried care.
He hands me a piece, and the weight of it settles into my hands like an old habit.
I check the action, sight along the barrel, and find it true.
It has been years since I last held a rifle in earnest.
They kept Tom and me well back of the guns through the war, shepherding the gentlemen of the press from one safe billet to the next.
But the musketry stayed in the hands, and I always did have the eye for it.
I shall not embarrass myself today.
The others are already gathering on the lawn.
Doctor Baldwin stands a little apart, grey and unwell.
Samuel Manning is a sorry sight, swaying where he stands.
Lady Claythorn is the last to come out.
And the Captain. Upright. Correct.
A decorated officer at his ease, at least in appearance.
Butler: Good, now that everyone is ready, I propose we split into two groups.
Butler: It will be easier to spot game that way.
Sushil Sinha: If I may, my lady. I should consider it a privilege to accompany you.
Lady Claythorn: How gallant of you, Captain. The privilege, I assure you, is mine.
Samuel Manning: Doctor, I would be honoured to partner with you.
Samuel Manning: You don't mind, do you?
Doctor Baldwin's mouth opens and closes again. He plainly minds a great deal, yet he cannot find the words to refuse.
Daniel Baldwin: Well... no, of course not.
That settles one party, then. The doctor, the drunkard, and a footman to mind the pair of them.
Which leaves our hostess and the Captain wanting a third gun for the other.
I do not mean to leave that to chance.
Thomas Moody: Doctor Baldwin and Mr Manning will do well enough together.
Thomas Moody: I should be glad to make up your party, my lady, if you will have me.
Lady Claythorn: Why, Mr Moody, of course.
Lady Claythorn: The more the merrier.
Sushil Sinha: A pleasure, Mr Moody.
Thomas Moody: The pleasure is entirely mine, Captain.
He returns my courtesy without a flicker.
He has no notion of what I carry in my pocket, nor what I mean to make of the morning.
Good.
Butler: Very good. Doctor Baldwin and Mr Manning to the western grove.
Butler: The footman will go along with them.
Butler: My lady, Captain Sinha and Mr Moody to the north field, and I shall attend.
We walk some distance before the first quarry breaks cover. A pheasant, not ten yards off our line.
I have it before the Captain's rifle is even at his shoulder.
The bird drops cleanly.
Lady Claythorn: Bravo, Mr Moody. A splendid shot.
Thomas Moody: You flatter me, my lady.
Thomas Moody: One does one's best.
The Captain's turn comes a few minutes on, at a rabbit sat in the grass.
He misses by a yard, and not for want of a steady morning.
A decorated soldier, and he cannot put a ball into a rabbit sitting still in the open.
I had wondered at it. Now I am sure.
Whatever he was in the war, it was not what he tells the dinner table.
Thomas Moody: Hard luck, Captain. Hard luck indeed.
Sushil Sinha: I misjudged the lead.
Thomas Moody: Quite. The lead.
I let a little of the mockery show. I cannot help myself.
So this is the great Captain Sinha. A man who cannot strike a rabbit at twenty paces, who holds forth each evening on Burma and the Boxers as though he had won the Empire single-handed.
A counterfeit hero in a borrowed reputation. The very hand that signed Tom away to the guns, and then dined out, year upon year, on a war he never once smelt.
The anger rises in my throat like bile.
Lady Claythorn fares no better with her piece, dipping the barrel and handling it like a parasol.
But it is the Captain my eye keeps returning to.
There is not an honest article in this whole party.
Myself least of all.
By the time the butler calls us in for luncheon, only I have any game to show for the morning.
Lady Claythorn: Three birds and a pair of rabbits, Mr Moody. You are most impressive.
Lady Claythorn: I confess I had no notion we should be so splendidly provided for.
Thomas Moody: Your ladyship is too generous.
Thomas Moody: I was simply very lucky today, that is all.
Thomas Moody: Otherwise, I am certain a decorated veteran like Captain Sinha would have put me quite to shame.
The Captain says nothing to that. He only smiles, thin and correct.
We settle for luncheon in a clearing among the birches. The butler lays a cloth and pours the tea with his customary care.
Lady Claythorn: Captain, you simply must tell us something of your soldiering.
Lady Claythorn: We have a real campaigner among us, and here I am letting him eat in silence.
Sushil Sinha: There is little worth the telling, my lady.
Sushil Sinha: But if it would amuse you, I might give you the relief of Tientsin. The heat of it, and the walls.
And he is away, modest and precise, with the polished ease of a man who has told it across a hundred dinner tables.
Lady Claythorn leans in, delighted.
I sit very still and let it wash over me.
This man held the safest billet in France, and came home to play the hero over the sandwiches, while Tom came home behind a mask and died by inches for the want of the very courage this fraud counterfeits so prettily.
My thumb finds the cold of the trigger guard where the rifle rests across my knee.
There is no doubt left in me to stay my hand.
Whatever game is being played beneath this roof, the name at the foot of that order is his, and Tom is in the ground.
That has always been enough.
I let the talk run on a little longer, and gather myself, and wait for my opening.
Thomas Moody: Captain, a word, if I may.
Thomas Moody: My rifle has been pulling to the left all morning.
Thomas Moody: I should value a second eye upon it.
Thomas Moody: A few paces up the track will do.
He hesitates, and a wiser man would have refused outright.
But Captain Sinha is a creature of manners before he is anything else, and manners will not let him deny so small a courtesy.
I have counted upon that all weekend.
It is the last thing his breeding will ever cost him.
Sushil Sinha: Very well.
Sushil Sinha: Lead on, Mr Moody.
Sushil Sinha: I shall be right behind you.
I lead him up the track, my pace unhurried, the very picture of a man with nothing on his mind but a wayward sight.
He follows a careful step behind, the way a man follows when half of him already knows.
Thirty yards on, where the cover thickens and the voices of the luncheon fall away, I judge it far enough.
I stop. I turn. I bring the rifle down from my shoulder.
Not to show him.
To hold.
Thomas Moody: That is quite far enough, I think, Captain.
I bring the barrel level with his chest.
His own rifle hangs at his side, a full yard further than his hand can reach in time.
I watch him understand it. I watch him decide against trying.
Sushil Sinha: Mr Moody.
Sushil Sinha: What is the meaning of this?
Thomas Moody: You see, Captain, I found a piece of paper upon my bed yesterday.
Thomas Moody: An old army order, written in the autumn of 1917. A transfer.
Thomas Moody: Until then, the officer named upon it had held the safest billet in France.
Thomas Moody: Conducting officer to the war correspondents, minding the gentlemen of the press well back of the guns, where no harm could come to him.
Thomas Moody: This order pulled him off it and sent him up to the line.
Thomas Moody: I assume you can guess his name: Thomas Moody.
Thomas Moody: And at the foot of the order, the name of the man who put his signature to it.
Thomas Moody: Captain S. Sinha, Staff Officer, General Headquarters.
Thomas Moody: A stroke of your pen, and a man was taken from safety and marched into the worst of the war.
Thomas Moody: Most who were sent up that season are in the Flanders mud yet.
Thomas Moody: And the few who came back were not much luckier.
Thomas Moody: They go through life as living monsters, hiding behind a mask such as this.
I tap the porcelain of my mask with two gloved fingers, as though the lie of it were the proof of everything.
Beneath it my face is whole. The mask was only ever a borrowed grief.
But the grief it stands for is mine, and it is real, and it has a name.
He has gone very still.
A clever man, weighing the paper against his memory and finding the memory wanting.
Sushil Sinha: You are mistaken, Mr Moody.
Sushil Sinha: I only ever signed what was laid before me, drafted by other hands.
Sushil Sinha: I never chose a single name, least of all his.
Thomas Moody: Ah! How convenient for you, Captain.
Thomas Moody: But I have no reason to believe you, in any case.
Thomas Moody: Especially since I am now sure that you are not the war hero you claim.
Thomas Moody: It was obvious today, when you could not even shoot a bird right under your nose.
Thomas Moody: Yet yesterday you were entertaining everyone with tales of Burma and the Boxers, as though you had fought in them yourself.
Thomas Moody: You are a fraud, that is clear enough.
Thomas Moody: And maybe somebody is using me to hurt you.
Thomas Moody: But I am very happy to oblige.
I do not lower the rifle.
Whatever he reads in my face, let him read it true. I have no wish to disguise it.
Sushil Sinha: Please, Mr Moody, consider what is at stake here.
Sushil Sinha: Someone here is manufacturing all of this.
Sushil Sinha: They were likely hoping for precisely this to happen.
Sushil Sinha: You are being used to their end.
Sushil Sinha: And you are no safer than I am.
Thomas Moody: How very considerate of you, Captain.
Thomas Moody: But I can quite look after myself, I assure you.
Sushil Sinha: Thomas. Please.
Sushil Sinha: It is all a mistake.
Thomas Moody: You will not sway me by using that name.
Thomas Moody: You will only anger me the more.
Sushil Sinha: What do you mean?
Thomas Moody: Well, I suppose there is no reason to hide it anymore.
Thomas Moody: Thomas was the name of someone very dear to me.
Thomas Moody: I would call him Tom, and he would call me Archie.
Thomas Moody: But that is over now.
Thomas Moody: All because of you.
He opens his mouth, to question it or to plead, I neither know nor care.
I raise the rifle to his head before the words can come.
His hands fly up, as though flesh and bone could turn a bullet aside.
Thomas Moody: Ah! Further proof you never saw battle, Captain.
Thomas Moody: Well.
Thomas Moody: This is as close as it gets.
The shot rolls away through the trees and is swallowed by them.
Captain Sinha lies where he fell, and there is nothing of the soldier left in him now.
I wait for the satisfaction of it. For something to fill the hollow that has sat in my chest since they put Tom in the ground.
It does not come.
There is only a dead man in the bracken, and my two hands, and the quiet.
Then, from the direction of the other party, another shot. And after it, faint and thin between the trees, a cry.
The doctor, or the drunkard, or both. It scarcely matters which.
Whoever drew us under this roof wanted blood this morning, and every one of us has proved so very willing to spill it.
Two men dead before the luncheon is cleared away, and the day not yet half gone.
I have no time for the horror of it. The shot will have carried back to the clearing, and they will come looking.
I compose my face behind the mask, take up my rifle, and start back the way we came.
A dreadful accident, I shall tell them. The Captain wandered ahead, and the cover was thick, and I never saw him.
God help me, I am becoming an easy liar.
