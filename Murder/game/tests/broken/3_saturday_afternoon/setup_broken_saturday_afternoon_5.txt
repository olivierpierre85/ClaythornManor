Chapter: The Hunt
Character: Thomas Moody
I return to my room to change into the tweeds the household has laid out for me.
My hands are steady as I dress. Steadier than they have any right to be.
The letter still lies on my bedside table.
If I am to believe it, Captain Sinha put his name to the order that sent Tom up to the line.
Because of that, Tom came back from the war behind a mask, and lived a small and lonely life until his wounds got the better of him.
A thought occurs to me, one I do not like but cannot ignore.
And a hunt is a careless sort of business.
Accidents happen.
The butler is in the gun room, attending the rifles with his unhurried care.
He hands me a rifle.
It has been years since I last held a rifle, yet it settles into my hands like an old habit.
I check the action, sight along the barrel, and find it true.
I shall not embarrass myself today.
The others are already gathering on the lawn.
Two different groups are forming: Captain Sinha with Lady Claythorn and Samuel Manning with Doctor Baldwin.
It looks like it is up to me to decide which party I will join.
My first instinct is to follow Captain Sinha.
I simply cannot ignore the letter.
Being close to him will allow me to learn more of it.
Yet, I do not trust what is happening here this weekend.
The letter, the hunt, the surprise the maid could not put a name to.
Making me follow the captain is probably the goal of whoever organised this.
So, which group should I join?
Menu Choice: Join Doctor Baldwin and Samuel Manning instead
Thomas Moody: Well, if it is all the same to everybody, I will go with Doctor Baldwin and Mr Manning.
The butler gives me an odd look, as if I ought to have shown a preference for our host's party.
By way of apology, I whisper to him.
Thomas Moody: I hope you'll forgive me, but I prefer not to leave Doctor Baldwin alone with Samuel Manning.
He nods in agreement and gives me a slight smile.
We strike off to the west, the doctor a few paces ahead, Mr Manning weaving along at my side.
Baldwin is grey and sweating, sunk in some private misery of his own, and pays neither of us any mind.
Samuel Manning's hands shake so badly he can scarcely hold his weapon.
And I am so busy observing the pair of them that I barely notice the rabbits and pheasants that come our way.
No wonder none of us has shot anything this morning.
We stop for lunch empty-handed.
Doctor Baldwin settles next to the footman, and Samuel Manning sits a little apart.
None of them seems eager for conversation.
Menu Choice: Sit down beside Samuel Manning
Thomas Moody: Mr Manning, do you mind if I sit here?
Samuel Manning: Oh, Mr Moody, no, not at all, of course.
I need to know if I can trust him, so I shouldn't waste time asking trivial questions.
If I want to know what is happening here, I need to be careful.
Menu Choice: Tell me more about yourself.
Thomas Moody: Tell me a little about yourself, Mr Manning.
Thomas Moody: What is your profession, if you don't mind my asking?
Samuel Manning: My profession.
Samuel Manning: Ha.
Samuel Manning: You are looking at a man of the law, sir.
Samuel Manning: A barrister.
Samuel Manning: Or... or what remains of one.
Thomas Moody: A barrister, no less.
Thomas Moody: What sort of practice did you keep?
Samuel Manning: Defence work.
Samuel Manning: I stood up for men the whole world had already hanged.
Samuel Manning: Somebody... somebody had to.
Thomas Moody: And why did you give it up?
Samuel Manning: Give it up.
Samuel Manning: Is that what I did.
Samuel Manning: There were cases, Mr Moody.
Samuel Manning: Cases that went... that went badly.
Samuel Manning: The law is a merciless thing when you get it wrong.
Samuel Manning: Leave it there, will you.
He takes a long pull from the flask and will not meet my eye.
A defence barrister.
An honourable job, but I am not sure it is one worthy of an award.
Menu Choice: Why were you invited here?
Thomas Moody: And why were you invited here, Mr Manning?
Samuel Manning: Well, I took the cases nobody else would touch, you see.
Samuel Manning: The poor.
Samuel Manning: The... the desperate.
Samuel Manning: A man of the people, the papers said.
Samuel Manning: Somebody has to speak for them, don't they?
Thomas Moody: That is a fine thing to be honoured for.
Samuel Manning: Honoured, that is a strong word to describe it.
And that is also a rather vague reason to be invited here.
Now that I think of it, so was Thomas's, actually.
More a general sort of praise than anything tied to a particular event.
Is the given reason for the award always vague?
Thomas Moody: Mr Manning, if you do not mind, can you tell me exactly what the letter said?
Samuel Manning: Letter, what letter? What do you know of a letter?
Thomas Moody: I mean the invitation letter.
Samuel Manning: Oh... Right, of course, well, it is like I said.
Samuel Manning: Due to your commitment to defending the poor and the needy, you were awarded the what's-its-name award.
Samuel Manning: Nothing special about it.
Vague reason of course, but what was that hesitation about a letter?
He clearly has another letter in mind, just as I do.
Menu Choice: I found something in my room last night. A letter.
There is one card left to me, and no clever way to play it.
So I lay it face up on the table.
Thomas Moody: Mr Manning, I am going to tell you something I have told nobody else in this house.
Thomas Moody: Last night I found something in my room. A letter.
Thomas Moody: No signature. Left where I could not fail to find it.
Thomas Moody: It told me of a wrong done to me by a man under this roof, and it was written to make me hate him.
The flask stops halfway to his mouth.
For a moment the drink seems to go clean out of him, and he stares at me as though I had reached into his skull.
Samuel Manning: A letter.
Samuel Manning: You as well.
Samuel Manning: That is... strange...
He searches my face, and whatever he finds there undoes him.
The words come out in a rush, as though he has been holding them since the small hours.
Samuel Manning: Mine told me what he did. The doctor.
Samuel Manning: He treated my Margaret, years ago. Held back the medicine that might have saved her, the very stuff he wanted for himself.
Samuel Manning: He is the reason she is in the ground.
Samuel Manning: But he was never condemned for it.
Samuel Manning: I cannot let him get away so easily. He has to pay.
So there it is.
The same hand that left an old army order upon my pillow left a letter for him, and aimed him at the doctor as it aimed me at the Captain.
We are not guests at all. We are loaded guns, laid out on a table for someone else to fire.
Well. I will not go off on command, and I will be damned if I let him do it either.
Thomas Moody: Listen to me, Mr Manning. Listen.
Thomas Moody: The same hand wrote your letter and mine. They want a death of us today, and they do not greatly care whose.
Thomas Moody: Do not give it to them.
Samuel Manning: But she... my Margaret...
Thomas Moody: I know. Believe me, I do.
Thomas Moody: But the man who has earned your bullet is not in this wood.
Thomas Moody: He is the one who held the pen.
Something in him gives way, like a sail when the wind drops.
The fight goes out of him, and what is left is only a tired and grieving old man, swaying in the bracken.
I take the rifle gently from his hands, and he lets me.
I do not leave his side for the rest of the halt.
The doctor walks out of the wood that afternoon, alive, and never once the wiser for how near a thing it was.
A man who was meant to die today is breathing, and a man who was meant to kill him is not a murderer. It is not much. But after this morning, I will take it.
