Chapter: The Hunt
Character: Sushil Sinha
I retire briefly to my room to change.
The household has laid out a tweed jacket of about my size.
I have brought my own but don't want to offend our hostess, so I put this one on.
Properly turned out, I make my way down to the gun room.
The butler is already there, attending to the rifles with unhurried care.
He hands me a piece, well-kept and finely balanced.
I weigh it briefly, then sight along the barrel and find it true.
It has been some years since I last shouldered such a weapon, yet the hands do not forget.
I have a passable hand at shooting. Not, perhaps, what my hosts expect of a decorated officer, but sufficient to avoid embarrassment.
I step out into the garden, where the others are already gathering.
Doctor Baldwin is checking the action of his rifle quietly.
Samuel Manning stands a little apart, a flask in one hand and a gun in the other. A poor pairing.
Mr Harring hovers at the edge of the group, plainly ill at ease. A footman attends him closely, as well he should.
Lady Claythorn is the last to emerge, dressed all in tweed.
At least, her outfit is perfect. But we will see if she can act the part.
Next to her, stands Thomas Moody.
He catches my eye and offers a small nod.
I return it. Steady. No hesitation.
Whatever he knows, or believes he knows, I will not give him the satisfaction of a flinch.
But I feel uneasy nevertheless.
Butler: Good, now that everyone is ready, I propose we split into two groups.
Butler: It will be easier to spot game that way.
A sensible proposal. Though the precise arrangement of the parties is a matter I shall not leave to chance.
Sushil Sinha: If I may, my lady. I should consider it a privilege to accompany you.
Lady Claythorn: How gallant of you, Captain. The privilege, I assure you, is mine.
Samuel Manning: Doctor, I would be honoured to partner with you.
Samuel Manning: You don't mind, do you?
Doctor Baldwin's mouth opens, then closes again. It is plain he does mind, but cannot find the words to refuse.
Daniel Baldwin: Well... no, of course not.
Mr Harring hovers at the edge of the gathering, plainly uncertain where he belongs.
He settles, in the end, with Mr Manning and Baldwin.
Thomas Moody: Come now, surely you shall have room for one more in the lady's party?
Thomas Moody: I should be sorry to miss our hostess's company.
Lady Claythorn: Why, Mr Moody, of course.
Lady Claythorn: The more the merrier.
A chill passes through me at his easy smile.
The letter on my bedside table.
The man who may have written it, now inviting himself into my shooting party.
I cannot object without drawing attention to myself.
Sushil Sinha: A pleasure, Mr Moody.
Thomas Moody: The pleasure is entirely mine, Captain.
The butler, who has watched the parties form with the unobtrusive attention of his trade, steps forward.
Butler: Very good. Doctor Baldwin and Mr Manning to the western grove, with Mr Harring.
Butler: The footman will go along with them.
Butler: My lady, Captain Sinha and Mr Moody to the north field, and I shall attend.
We walk some distance before the first quarry presents itself.
A pheasant, breaking cover not ten yards from our line.
Mr Moody's rifle cracks before mine is even at my shoulder.
The bird drops cleanly.
Lady Claythorn: Bravo, Mr Moody. A splendid shot.
Thomas Moody: You flatter me, my lady.
Thomas Moody: One does one's best.
I do not care for the ease of it.
That was not the shot of a man merely doing his best.
My own turn comes a few minutes later, at a rabbit in the heather.
My hands are not as steady as I should like.
The shot goes a full yard wide.
Thomas Moody: Hard luck, Captain. Hard luck indeed.
Thomas Moody: Though I confess I had expected rather better of a decorated officer.
Sushil Sinha: The light is most unhelpful today.
Thomas Moody: Quite. The light.
His tone carries the smallest edge, no more than a gentleman could credibly notice.
I feel it nonetheless.
Lady Claythorn: Oh, do leave the Captain in peace, Mr Moody.
Lady Claythorn: Not every gentleman cares to spend his weekends slaughtering birds.
A kind intervention, though offered a touch too late to count as kind.
We stop for luncheon in a clearing bordered by birches.
The butler lays out a spread upon a linen cloth and serves the tea with his customary care.
Mr Moody installs himself at Lady Claythorn's side and keeps up a steady flow of agreeable conversation.
I eat very little and say less.
Thomas Moody: Captain, you must come and see this.
Thomas Moody: There's a rather curious cairn a few paces off, up the track.
Thomas Moody: I wager you know your antiquities better than I do.
I have not the slightest interest in his cairn.
I have every interest in not being led alone into the trees.
Lady Claythorn: Do go, Captain. Mr Moody has quite the eye for these things.
The butler is tending to the horse at the edge of the clearing.
Lady Claythorn has already returned to her tea.
To refuse now would be conspicuous.
Sushil Sinha: Very well.
I follow him up the track.
He walks easily, his rifle slung at his shoulder, whistling a tune I do not recognise.
Thirty yards from the clearing, the cover thickens. The voices from the luncheon fall away.
He stops, turns, and lifts the rifle from his shoulder.
Not to show me. To hold.
Thomas Moody: That's far enough, I think, Captain.
The muzzle is not yet levelled, but the grip is wrong for a man out for rabbits.
My own rifle hangs at my side. There is a yard too many between us for me to bring it up in time.
Sushil Sinha: Mr Moody. What is the meaning of this?
Thomas Moody: The letter, Captain.
Thomas Moody: You did read it, I hope.
Thomas Moody: One hates to go to the trouble and find the recipient hasn't bothered.
Sushil Sinha: You wrote it.
Thomas Moody: Of course I wrote it.
Thomas Moody: An administrative officer, Captain. A man who spent the war behind a desk in Rawalpindi pushing paper about.
Thomas Moody: And here you are, entertaining the table with tales of Burma and the Boxers as though you had ever heard a shot fired in anger.
My mouth is dry.
I have stood in front of angry colonels and unpaid sepoys without flinching.
Yet here, in the quiet of a Scottish wood, I find I cannot speak.
Thomas Moody: It's rather plain you never saw battle, Captain.
Thomas Moody: Well. This is as close as it gets.
He lifts the rifle in one clean movement.
I raise a hand, uselessly.
You were led into the trees by a man who had taken the measure of you on Friday evening.
There was no battle in your past, and he had guessed as much.
Perhaps, if you had left your war stories in the drawing room, he would have had no reason to write that letter at all.
