Chapter: The Hunt
Character: Sushil Sinha
I retire briefly to my room to change.
The household has laid out a tweed jacket of about my size.
I have brought my own but don't want to offend our hostess, so I put this one on.
Properly turned out, I make my way down to the gun room.
The butler is already there, attending to the rifles with unhurried care.
He hands me a piece, well-kept and finely balanced.
I weigh it briefly, then sight along the barrel and find it true.
It has been some years since I last shouldered such a weapon, yet the hands do not forget.
I have a passable hand at shooting. Not, perhaps, what my hosts expect of a decorated officer, but sufficient to avoid embarrassment.
I step out into the garden, where the others are already gathering.
Doctor Baldwin is checking the action of his rifle quietly.
Samuel Manning stands a little apart, a flask in one hand and a gun in the other. A poor pairing.
Mr Harring hovers at the edge of the group, plainly ill at ease. A footman attends him closely, as well he should.
Lady Claythorn is the last to emerge, dressed all in tweed.
At least, her outfit is perfect. But we will see if she can act the part.
Next to her, stands Thomas Moody.
He catches my eye and offers a small nod.
I return it. Steady. No hesitation.
Whatever he knows, or believes he knows, I will not give him the satisfaction of a flinch.
But I feel uneasy nevertheless.
Butler: Good, now that everyone is ready, I propose we split into two groups.
Butler: It will be easier to spot game that way.
A sensible proposal. Though the precise arrangement of the parties is a matter I shall not leave to chance.
Sushil Sinha: If I may, my lady. I should consider it a privilege to accompany you.
Lady Claythorn: How gallant of you, Captain. The privilege, I assure you, is mine.
Samuel Manning: Doctor, I would be honoured to partner with you.
Samuel Manning: You don't mind, do you?
Doctor Baldwin's mouth opens, then closes again. It is plain he does mind, but cannot find the words to refuse.
Daniel Baldwin: Well... no, of course not.
Mr Harring hovers at the edge of the gathering, plainly uncertain where he belongs.
He settles, in the end, with Mr Manning and Baldwin.
Thomas Moody: Doctor Baldwin's party is already three guns strong.
Thomas Moody: I shall round out your own, if my lady will have me.
Lady Claythorn: Why, Mr Moody, of course.
Lady Claythorn: The more the merrier.
A chill passes through me.
The letter on my bedside table.
The man who may have written it, now inviting himself into my shooting party.
I cannot object without drawing attention to myself.
Sushil Sinha: A pleasure, Mr Moody.
Thomas Moody: The pleasure is entirely mine, Captain.
The butler steps forward.
Butler: Very good. Doctor Baldwin and Mr Manning to the western grove, with Mr Harring.
Butler: The footman will go along with them.
Butler: My lady, Captain Sinha and Mr Moody to the north field, and I shall attend.
We walk some distance before the first quarry presents itself.
A pheasant, breaking cover not ten yards from our line.
Mr Moody's rifle cracks before mine is even at my shoulder.
The bird drops cleanly.
Lady Claythorn: Bravo, Mr Moody. A splendid shot.
Thomas Moody: You flatter me, my lady.
Thomas Moody: One does one's best.
I do not care for the ease of it.
Mr Moody must be a remarkable marksman. His modesty is a performance.
My own turn comes a few minutes later, at a rabbit in the grass.
My hands are not as steady as I should like.
The shot goes a full yard wide.
Thomas Moody: Hard luck, Captain. Hard luck indeed.
Thomas Moody: Though I confess I had expected rather better of a decorated officer.
Sushil Sinha: I misjudged the lead.
Thomas Moody: Quite. The lead.
He is saying this playfully, but I cannot help but feel slighted.
The morning wears on in this fashion.
More birds burst from the bushes, and Mr Moody takes them without appearing to aim.
Lady Claythorn's shots go wide.
My own find nothing but empty air.
By the time the butler calls us in, only Mr Moody has game to show.
Lady Claythorn: Three birds and a pair of rabbits, Mr Moody. You are most impressive.
Lady Claythorn: I confess I had no notion we should be so splendidly provided for.
Thomas Moody: Your ladyship is too generous.
Thomas Moody: I was just very lucky today, that is all.
Thomas Moody: Otherwise, I am certain a decorated veteran like Captain Sinha would have put me quite to shame.
He says it politely enough, but I feel the mockery in his tone.
I force a thin smile and say no more.
We settle for luncheon in a clearing bordered by birches.
The butler lays out a spread upon a linen cloth and serves the tea with his customary care.
Mr Moody installs himself at Lady Claythorn's side and keeps up a steady flow of agreeable conversation.
I eat very little and say less.
Thomas Moody: Captain, a word, if I may.
Thomas Moody: My rifle has been pulling to the left all morning. I should value a second eye upon it.
Thomas Moody: A few paces up the track will do.
I don't like the idea of being led alone into the trees.
And it's hard to believe there is anything wrong with his gun.
But I can't find a proper reason not to go.
My manners overrule my better judgement.
Sushil Sinha: Very well.
Sushil Sinha: Go on, Mr Moody. I shall be behind you.
I follow him up the track.
He walks easily, his rifle slung at his shoulder, whistling a tune I do not recognise.
Thirty yards from the clearing, the cover thickens.
The voices from the luncheon fall away.
He stops, turns, and lifts the rifle from his shoulder.
Not to show me. To hold.
Thomas Moody: That's far enough, I think, Captain.
I have his gun now pointing straight at me.
Sushil Sinha: Mr Moody. What is the meaning of this?
My own rifle hangs at my side. There is a yard too many between us for me to bring it up in time.
Thomas Moody: I found a letter on my bed yesterday.
Thomas Moody: It told an interesting story.
Thomas Moody: One about an administrative officer from India, a man who spent his wars pushing paper about.
Thomas Moody: I believe you recognise whose story this is, Captain.
Thomas Moody: Yet here you are, entertaining the table with tales of Burma and the Boxers as though you had fought in them yourself.
Thomas Moody: But the true sting of it, Captain, lay in the final page.
Thomas Moody: Owing to a blatant failure of communication, a column was sent up an open ridge in the autumn of '17.
Thomas Moody: Three hundred men were cut down in the space of an afternoon, and many more gravely wounded.
Thomas Moody: Some were left so disfigured that they must wear masks such as this for the rest of their days.
He taps the porcelain of his mask with two gloved fingers.
Thomas Moody: Their lives were ruined forever.
Thomas Moody: And the letter was careful to note who signed the wrong order.
Thomas Moody: You shall not be surprised to learn it was you, Captain.
Thomas Moody: So many innocent victims, because of your incompetence.s
My mouth is dry.
I have stood in front of angry colonels without flinching.
Yet here, in the quiet of a Scottish wood, I find the words stuck in my throat.
I have signed a great many papers in my time. Transfers, requisitions, routine dispatches.
Most of them I could not recall if my life depended upon it.
Yet I cannot believe I am the man his letter describes.
Amid all the horrors of those years, a sin of that magnitude would not have slipped my memory.
Whatever happened to him, I am not responsible. I am sure of that.
Sushil Sinha: That is a slander, Mr Moody.
Sushil Sinha: I have never sent men to their deaths by mistake.
Sushil Sinha: I am in no way responsible for your... for your... condition.
He does not lower the rifle.
His gaze is resolute, heavy with a hatred.
I see now there is nothing I can say that will change his mind.
Sushil Sinha: Then you were the one who placed the letter in my room.
Sushil Sinha: The one accusing me.
Thomas Moody: A letter in your room?
Thomas Moody: No, that was not me.
Thomas Moody: Why would I have done something so foolish?
Thomas Moody: It would only have tipped you off.
Sushil Sinha: Then consider what its purpose is.
Sushil Sinha: Someone here is manufacturing all of this.
Sushil Sinha: He was likely hoping for precisely this to happen.
Sushil Sinha: You are being used to his end.
Sushil Sinha: And you are no safer than I.
Thomas Moody: How very considerate of you, Captain.
Thomas Moody: I can quite look after myself, I assure you.
He levels the rifle at my head.
I raise a hand, uselessly.
Thomas Moody: It is rather plain you never saw battle, Captain.
Thomas Moody: Well. This is as close as it gets.
You were led into the woods by a man who had cultivated a deep hatred for you.
You knew the danger, yet good manners compelled you to proceed regardless.
It is curious how the fear of causing a scene can prove stronger than any other.
