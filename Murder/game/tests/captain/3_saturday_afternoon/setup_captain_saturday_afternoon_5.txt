Chapter: The Hunt
Character: Sushil Sinha
I retire briefly to my room to change.
The household has laid out a tweed jacket of about my size.
I have brought my own but don't want to offend our hostess, so I put this one on.
Properly turned out, I make my way down to the gun room.
The butler is already there, attending to the rifles with unhurried care.
He hands me a piece, well-kept and finely balanced.
I weigh it briefly, then sight along the barrel and find it true.
It has been some years since I last shouldered such a weapon, yet the hands do not forget.
I have a passable hand at shooting. Not, perhaps, what my hosts expect of a decorated officer, but sufficient to avoid embarrassment.
I step out into the garden, where the others are already gathering.
Doctor Baldwin is checking the action of his rifle quietly.
Samuel Manning stands a little apart, a flask in one hand and a gun in the other. A poor pairing.
Mr Harring hovers at the edge of the group, plainly ill at ease. A footman attends him closely, as well he should.
Lady Claythorn is the last to emerge, dressed all in tweed.
At least, her outfit is perfect. But we will see if she can act the part.
Butler: Good, now that everyone is ready, I propose we split into two groups.
Butler: It will be easier to spot game that way.
A sensible proposal. Though the precise arrangement of the parties is a matter I shall not leave to chance.
Sushil Sinha: If I may, my lady. I should consider it a privilege to accompany you.
Lady Claythorn: How gallant of you, Captain. The privilege, I assure you, is mine.
Samuel Manning: Doctor, I would be honoured to partner with you.
Samuel Manning: You don't mind, do you?
Doctor Baldwin's mouth opens, then closes again. It is plain he does mind, but cannot find the words to refuse.
Daniel Baldwin: Well... no, of course not.
Mr Harring hovers at the edge of the gathering, plainly uncertain where he belongs.
He settles, in the end, with Mr Manning and Baldwin.
The butler, who has been listening with the unobtrusive attention of his trade, steps forward.
Butler: If I may, my lady.
Butler: Doctor Baldwin and Mr Manning to the western grove, with Mr Harring.
Butler: The footman will go along with them.
Butler: My lady and Captain Sinha to the north field, and I shall attend.
Good, a morning in our hostess's company is definitely the better outcome for me.
Whatever she conceals, a few hours alone may loosen her tongue.
We walk for some time through the undergrowth before coming across anything worth a shot.
Lady Claythorn carries her piece as though it were a parasol that had grown unaccountably heavy.
Twice the barrel has dipped toward the earth. Once she has shifted her grip in plain sight, as if she could not recall where her hands belonged.
A pheasant breaks cover. She brings the rifle up late, the stock set too high against her shoulder.
The shot goes wide by a great margin. The recoil jolts her visibly, and she winces before she thinks to hide it.
She laughs it off with a small, embarrassed shake of the head.
Lady Claythorn: The light is most unhelpful today.
The light is perfectly fine.
She has scarcely handled a rifle in her life. That much is plain.
A gentlewoman who has organised a hunting weekend on her own grounds ought to know one end of her gun from the other.
A rabbit bolts from the fern.
I raise, aim, and fire.
A clean miss, though not by much.
I lower the rifle and allow myself a small, private smile.
Lady Claythorn: Captain, that was a nice attempt.
Lady Claythorn: I am sure you will have better luck next time.
Sushil Sinha: You are too kind, my lady.
Sushil Sinha: I suppose you were right about the light.
Well, if I must keep my cover, I might as well go along with her lies.
Nothing else comes our way, and soon it is time to stop for luncheon.
The butler lays out a modest spread on a linen cloth, pours tea, then steps back with a brief bow.
Butler: If you don't mind, my lady. I should like to look in upon the other party, that they might not want for anything.
Butler: I shall be back soon.
Lady Claythorn: Very well. Thank you.
Finally, we are alone.
The moment I was hoping for.
A rifle across my knees. The butler away.
It may not come again.
I feel like I have seen enough to know "Lady Claythorn" is not who she appears to be.
And yet, I will have to suppress everything in my education if I want to tell her that.
It does not help that she has a loaded weapon beside her.
Confronting her now might not be the wisest course of action.
Menu Choice: Press her on who she really is
I set down my cup with deliberate care and allow the rifle to rest within easier reach.
Opportunity will not wait upon a man's second thoughts.
Sushil Sinha: Lady Claythorn. Forgive the indelicacy.
Sushil Sinha: There is a matter I should like to put to you plainly.
Lady Claythorn: Goodness, Captain, how grave you sound.
Lady Claythorn: Whatever can be the matter?
Sushil Sinha: I notice a lot of things that do not make sense this weekend:
Sushil Sinha: Your portrait is nowhere in the gallery.
Sushil Sinha: You either do not know, or do not follow, proper dining etiquette.
Sushil Sinha: The hunt you yourself arranged appears to be quite beyond your skill.
Sushil Sinha: And, worst of all, the surname you are using is not your title, as it should be.
Sushil Sinha: I do not believe you are Lady Claythorn.
Sushil Sinha: In fact, I do not think there is a "Lady Claythorn."
Her smile holds.
Firmly, as a practised hostess will hold a smile through a guest's poor taste.
Lady Claythorn: Captain, I confess myself astonished.
Lady Claythorn: The morning sun has plainly done your head a mischief.
Lady Claythorn: You are clearly confusing yourself with some detective from a poorly written novel.
Lady Claythorn: There is a perfectly good explanation for everything you have mentioned.
Sushil Sinha: Well, in that case, please enlighten me.
Lady Claythorn: Shall we take them in turn, Captain?
Lady Claythorn: As for my portrait, I have never cared to sit for one.
Lady Claythorn: Its absence in the gallery is my own doing, nothing more sinister than vanity in reverse.
Lady Claythorn: For my manners at table, I live here for the most part alone.
Lady Claythorn: When one dines without company for months on end, the old conventions grow dusty.
Lady Claythorn: And for the hunt, I arranged it for the pleasure of my guests, not my own.
Lady Claythorn: I take up a rifle perhaps once a year.
Lady Claythorn: The light was unkind to me this morning, but I confess that even in fair weather I should be no credit to my name.
Lady Claythorn: Now, for the name itself, my late husband had grown weary of ceremony, as so many did after the war.
Lady Claythorn: He preferred that we be addressed by the house rather than the peerage.
Lady Claythorn: A small indulgence, perhaps. Hardly a conspiracy.
Each answer, taken on its own, is perfectly plausible.
Worse than that. Each echoes the very explanations I myself had turned over in my mind when I first noticed these things.
But the answers come too readily, as if rehearsed.
An honest woman would stumble. Take offence. Demand to know what on earth I meant by it all.
Instead, she has submitted to my interrogation as if she were prepared for it.
But let's see how well she is really prepared.
Sushil Sinha: That is a very logical explanation for everything.
Sushil Sinha: But in that case, what is your title?
Sushil Sinha: If you are right, you cannot have forgotten it.
There, she hesitates.
Lady Claythorn: My memory, I am afraid, is not what it once was.
Sushil Sinha: Surely, my Lady would know her title.
Sushil Sinha: There is no doubt your father has repeated it constantly as you were growing up.
Sushil Sinha: You should have heard it said by friends, by visitors, in letters addressed to you.
Sushil Sinha: You must have heard it when you were presented at court.
She prepares to answer, but cannot find the words.
Finally, her smile fades by degrees, and something tired and older takes its place beneath it.
Lady Claythorn: Very well, Captain.
Lady Claythorn: You are right.
Lady Claythorn: I am not Lady Claythorn.
Lady Claythorn: I am just playing the part.
Sushil Sinha: But why? What is the purpose of this?
Lady Claythorn: That, I do not know.
Lady Claythorn: I was hired for a role, an unconventional one I admit.
Lady Claythorn: But all I was told was what to do, not the reason behind it.
Sushil Sinha: Hired? By whom?
Lady Claythorn: The arrangement was made through a firm of solicitors in London.
Lady Claythorn: I never met the person behind this enterprise.
Lady Claythorn: The pay was generous, so I didn't ask many questions.
It takes me a while to take everything in.
That story goes way beyond what I had imagined.
It leads to even more questions.
Sushil Sinha: And the letter left in my room last night?
Lady Claythorn: What letter?
Lady Claythorn: I know nothing of any letter, Captain.
Sushil Sinha: And Thomas Moody?
Sushil Sinha: A man is found dead in his bed, and you carry on as though a guest had spilt his wine.
Lady Claythorn: I have no idea what happened to him.
Lady Claythorn: That was never planned!
She shifts as she speaks, her weight settling then settling again, as though the ground beneath her would not hold still.
Sushil Sinha: I am sorry to say, madam, but I do not believe you.
I lift the rifle and level it at her.
Not aiming, precisely. Simply making it plain that, at this small clearing, I am the one asking the questions.
Her eyes widen. She sets her cup down with a slow, careful hand.
Lady Claythorn: Captain. Please.
Sushil Sinha: Now, you will explain clearly the terms of the arrangement.
Sushil Sinha: I want to know everything.
She opens her mouth to answer.
A twig snaps behind me.
I begin to turn, far too slowly.
Something draws tight around my throat.
The rifle is struck cleanly from my hands before I can bring it round.
A broad forearm crosses my shoulders.
A thin leather strap crushes against my windpipe.
I drive an elbow backwards and find nothing but heavy tweed.
The butler's breath is steady against my ear.
Lady Claythorn: Wait.
Lady Claythorn: There is no need to hurt him.
Butler: I am afraid there is.
Butler: I've heard what he said to you.
Butler: He knows too much.
My vision blurs at the edges.
I grope for the knife at my belt.
My fingers will not close upon it.
A moment more, and the forest folds inward and goes dark.
You pressed your hostess at the wrong moment, in the wrong place.
You felt safe with your gun next to you.
But a rifle is a poor weapon when the threat comes silently from behind.
Next time you want to confront someone, choose your ground wisely.
