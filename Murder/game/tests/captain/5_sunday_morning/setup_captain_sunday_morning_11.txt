Chapter: An Empty Manor
Character: Sushil Sinha
I sleep poorly, and only in patches.
The house stays quiet through the night.
A little before half past eight, I dress and sit on the edge of the bed.
There is a soft, quick knock at the door.
Rosalind Marsh: Captain. It's me.
I unlock the door.
Miss Marsh slips inside without a word and closes it behind her.
She is pale, but quite collected.
Sushil Sinha: Miss Marsh.
Sushil Sinha: Are you all right?
Rosalind Marsh: I am, but there is something wrong with the house.
Sushil Sinha: What do you mean?
Rosalind Marsh: There are no staff. No fires lit. No breakfast laid.
Rosalind Marsh: I have walked the dining room, the kitchen, the back corridor.
Rosalind Marsh: Not a soul.
Sushil Sinha: So I was right, something strange is happening here.
Sushil Sinha: Have you checked any of the other rooms?
Rosalind Marsh: No, I came straight to you.
Sushil Sinha: A sound choice.
Sushil Sinha: It might not be safe for you to just roam around the house.
Rosalind Marsh: I believe you are right.
Rosalind Marsh: I was thinking we might hide for a while, at least until the police come.
Sushil Sinha: Right, the police.
Sushil Sinha: I do not know for sure they will come, Miss Marsh.
Sushil Sinha: There is a good chance they were never called in the first place.
Rosalind Marsh: Never called, but Lady Claythorn...
Rosalind Marsh: Oh...
Sushil Sinha: Yes, if Lady Claythorn is behind this masquerade, and there is a very good chance that she is, there was no reason for her to call the police.
Sushil Sinha: We might wait here indefinitely.
Rosalind Marsh: What can we do then?
Sushil Sinha: The way I see it, we have two choices.
Sushil Sinha: We can search the house and try to make sense of what is happening, or we can leave at once and make for the town.
Somewhere below us, a door bangs.
Then footsteps. More than one set, moving fast.
Rosalind Marsh: Captain...
I raise a hand for silence and listen.
Floorboards along the corridor below. A voice, too muffled to make out. Another door.
Whoever they are, they are going through the house room by room.
Sushil Sinha: Someone is searching the house.
Sushil Sinha: I should go out and meet them. Better to face a man than be cornered by one.
Rosalind Marsh: Captain, no.
Rosalind Marsh: We do not know who is out there.
Rosalind Marsh: It might be one of the guests. It might be whoever did away with the staff.
Rosalind Marsh: If you open that door, you will find out which only when it is too late.
I weigh it.
I have one revolver, six rounds, and no notion of their numbers or their intent.
She is right. The arithmetic is against me.
Sushil Sinha: Very well, we stay out of sight.
Sushil Sinha: But not here.
Sushil Sinha: If they are opening doors, they will reach this room sooner or later.
Rosalind Marsh: The attic.
Rosalind Marsh: The staff are gone, and no guest has any business up there.
Rosalind Marsh: Nobody will think to look for us amongst empty servants' rooms.
Sushil Sinha: The attic, good.
Sushil Sinha: I have the butler's master key, so the locked doors up there are no obstacle.
Sushil Sinha: We wait for the corridor to clear, then we move. Quickly and quietly.
We stand on either side of the door and listen.
The footsteps pass along the corridor, pause somewhere at the far end, and fade towards the stairs.
I ease the door open.
The corridor is empty.
We make for the attic stair, Miss Marsh ahead, my eyes on the landing behind us.
The narrow stair creaks under our weight, and then we are up amongst the servants' doors.
I fit the master key to the butler's room. It belonged to the head man of the house, and it has the soundest door up here.
I lock the door behind us.
A narrow bed, a washstand, a single chair. Against the far wall, a heavy glass-panelled cabinet with the household silver asleep inside it.
Miss Marsh takes the chair. I set my back against the wall where I can watch the door.
Sushil Sinha: Now we wait.
Sushil Sinha: If the house stays quiet until midday, we will decide our next move.
The minutes settle in around us.
Miss Marsh sits very straight, her hands folded in her lap, listening as I am listening.
It is a long time to sit in silence with a stranger.
Menu Choice: Say nothing
Neither of us has any appetite for conversation.
We sit with our own thoughts, and the house settles around us.
No one has tried the attic stair.
The footsteps below stopped a long while ago, and the house has gone back to its heavy silence.
Sushil Sinha: Miss Marsh, I have been thinking.
Sushil Sinha: The police are not coming. Nobody is coming.
Sushil Sinha: Someone has to walk to the town and bring back help, and it had better be done in daylight.
Rosalind Marsh: Walk?
Rosalind Marsh: It must be ten miles to the town, Captain.
Sushil Sinha: It is a fair distance, but there are hours of daylight left.
Sushil Sinha: If we set out now, we can be there before dark.
Rosalind Marsh: Oh, I am sure you could, Captain.
Rosalind Marsh: Sadly I can't.
She says it quietly.
I see now what I had been too preoccupied to mark before.
The pallor that is not only fear.
The careful way she holds herself.
Rosalind Marsh: I am not a healthy woman, Captain.
Rosalind Marsh: There is a good chance my body will not hold for such a long journey.
Sushil Sinha: I see.
I think her condition might be worse than her quiet behaviour shows.
But if she does not want to tell me more, I will not press her.
Sushil Sinha: Then it is decided. I go alone, and you stay here.
Sushil Sinha: This room has a sound door and a sound lock.
Sushil Sinha: I shall come back with help before the day is out.
Rosalind Marsh: I see.
Rosalind Marsh: If I am being honest, I do not like this, Captain.
Rosalind Marsh: It feels extremely dangerous for me.
Sushil Sinha: I know and I am sorry, but I still believe it is our best chance.
Sushil Sinha: And if it can ease your mind, you can take this pistol.
Sushil Sinha: I try to always keep it on me, one never knows when one might need protection.
Sushil Sinha: Do you know how to use one?
Rosalind Marsh: I can manage, I believe.
Rosalind Marsh: But I really hope I won't have to use it.
Sushil Sinha: You probably won't.
Sushil Sinha: Lock the door behind me and do not open to anybody.
Sushil Sinha: It is safe to say that everyone is a suspect at this point.
I hand her the pistol, and the master key with it.
I do not need the key anymore anyway.
Rosalind Marsh: All right, I think I can manage that.
Rosalind Marsh: Thank you, Captain.
Sushil Sinha: Great, in that case I see no reason to linger.
Sushil Sinha: I will go straight away and come back as soon as possible.
Sushil Sinha: Good luck, Miss Marsh.
Rosalind Marsh: Good luck, Captain.
I do not waste time and go down fast to the entrance hall.
It is still empty, so I make it outside.
I take only my coat and what will fit in its pockets.
The drive gives way to a rough road, and the road to open country between the trees.
I keep a solid pace for an hour, then, away to my right, I notice something.
A shape that is the wrong colour for bark.
And from that shape, I can spot the barrel of a rifle.
I quickly grab for my own gun but come up empty.
Miss Marsh has it.
I am defenceless.
You set out alone, on foot, into open country you did not know.
A soldier ought to have known better than to cross such ground without cover.
But you were no soldier, whatever the medals said.
The shot came from the treeline, and you did not hear the second one.
